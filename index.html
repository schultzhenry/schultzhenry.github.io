116th

2018

In partial fulfillment of Yale's Graphic Design Methodologies course with Pamela Hovland, I designed this zine to celebrate the historic representation of women in the 116th Congress.

The body of the zine lists all congressional representatives from the 1st Congress, which began meeting in 1789, to the 116th Congress, elected in 2018. Names of women are printed in green.

10 copies of the zine were printed for the Odds and Ends Art Book Fair.

Engender

2016-now

In 2016, events involving race and gender-based discrimination motivated a group of classmates and I to correct structural inequalities on our campus. To organize around this goal, we founded Engender.

Currently, Engender advocates for reforming Greek organizations given their discriminatory nature and disproportionate control over undergraduate social life. The existing structure of these organizations enables harmful behaviors including hazing, substance abuse, sexual misconduct, and acts of hate that occur frequently on American college campuses to the detriment of students' mental and physical wellbeing. Our organization advocates for better oversight of these organizations as well as membership policies designed to celebrate rather than punish difference.

My personal responsibilities for Engender have included communications, identity, branding, member onboarding, and organizing a group of women and nonbinary students to rush a fraternity.

See Engender's website, Facebook or Twitter.

Fuzzy Grids II

2014

While interning at Predock_Frane architects, I helped design and fabricate Fuzzy Grids II, an interactive art installation at the Getty Center.

Fuzzy Grids II is a field of colorful large-scale cubes set on the grid of the Getty Center's Arrival Plaza. The white boxes reference the monochrome surfaces of Richard Meier's architecture, while the color is sampled from the polychromatic Robert Irwin-designed garden. In collaboration with sound artist Chris Rountree, who supplies an idiosyncratic soundtrack to guide participants with different tempos and atmospheres, Predock_Frane Architects have designed a flexible kit of parts that visitors can manipulate—like a giant game board—to form patterns, images, and even three-dimensional landscapes.

— The J. Paul Getty Museum
